# Exercise for Neurodivergent Users

This guide explains how to exercise safely and enjoyably for people with autism, Down syndrome, and cerebral palsy. All exercises are described in plain language.

---

## General Principles for Neurodivergent Fitness

1. **Always warm up first** — 5–10 minutes of gentle movement before exercise helps the body get ready and reduces injury risk.
2. **Cool down at the end** — 5 minutes of gentle stretching after exercise helps the body relax and recover.
3. **Rest is important** — Rest days are part of the program. The body needs time to recover and get stronger.
4. **Listen to your body** — If something hurts or feels very uncomfortable, stop. Pain is a signal to rest.
5. **Start small** — Even 10 minutes of movement is excellent. Build up slowly over weeks and months.
6. **Have fun** — If an exercise feels boring or unpleasant, try a different one. NuroCoach can always suggest alternatives.

## Exercises for People with Autism

People with autism may have sensory sensitivities, prefer routines, or find certain environments overwhelming. Good exercise options include:

### Sensory-Friendly Activities
- **Walking** — A calm, predictable activity. Try walking at the same time each day to build routine. Choose quiet paths or parks to avoid sensory overload.
- **Swimming** — Deep pressure from water can feel calming. Use earplugs if sounds are overwhelming.
- **Yoga** — Slow, structured movements with clear steps. Choose beginner-level videos with simple instructions.
- **Trampolining** — Many autistic individuals enjoy the proprioceptive (body awareness) input from bouncing. Use a safe, enclosed trampoline.
- **Cycling** — Stationary bikes are a good option for a predictable, low-sensory environment.

### Tips for Exercise with Autism
- Use **visual schedules** to show what the workout will look like before starting.
- Keep **routines consistent** — same time, same place when possible.
- Offer **headphones or earplugs** if the environment is noisy.
- Allow **stimming** (self-stimulatory behaviour) before and after exercise to help regulate.
- Use **countdown timers** so the person knows how long each activity lasts.
- Celebrate every effort, not just outcomes.

## Exercises for People with Down Syndrome

People with Down syndrome often have lower muscle tone (hypotonia), flexible joints, and may be at risk for Atlanto-Axial Instability (AAI) — a condition affecting the neck. This means some activities need modification.

### Recommended Activities
- **Walking and hiking** — Excellent for cardiovascular health and is low-risk.
- **Swimming** — Gentle on joints, great for building strength and cardiovascular fitness.
- **Dancing** — Fun, rhythmic, and great for coordination. Use simple, repetitive movements.
- **Resistance band exercises** — Gentle strength training that is easy to modify.
- **Balance exercises** — Standing on one foot, walking along a line, or using a balance board (with support) helps coordination.
- **Stretching routines** — Important because of joint hypermobility. Focus on controlled, gentle stretches.

### Important Safety Notes for Down Syndrome
- **Always check with a doctor** about Atlanto-Axial Instability (AAI) before starting. If AAI is present, avoid exercises that involve the neck bending forward intensely (e.g., somersaults, diving, gymnastics, contact sports).
- Avoid **high-impact activities** (like jumping on hard surfaces) if joint pain is present.
- **Heart screening** is recommended before beginning a new exercise program as some people with Down syndrome have congenital heart conditions.
- Monitor for **overheating** — people with Down syndrome may have reduced ability to regulate body temperature during exercise.

### Example Beginner Routine (Down Syndrome)
1. **Warm Up (5 min):** Gentle arm circles, shoulder shrugs, leg swings holding a chair for support
2. **Walking (15 min):** Comfortable pace outdoors or on a treadmill
3. **Seated Resistance Band Rows (2 sets x 10 reps):** Sit in a chair, hold a resistance band, pull it toward your chest
4. **Standing Balance (2 x 30 seconds each side):** Hold a chair, lift one foot slightly off the ground
5. **Cool Down (5 min):** Seated hamstring stretch, neck side stretch (gentle), deep breathing

## Exercises for People with Cerebral Palsy

Cerebral palsy affects movement and muscle tone differently for each person. Some people have mild effects; others need more support. Always adapt exercises to what the individual can comfortably do.

### Recommended Activities
- **Hydrotherapy / Water Exercise** — Water supports body weight, making movement easier. Excellent for all CP types.
- **Seated Exercise** — Many strength, flexibility, and cardio exercises can be done from a chair or wheelchair.
- **Stretching** — Spasticity (muscle stiffness) is common with CP. Regular gentle stretching helps maintain range of motion.
- **Adaptive Cycling** — Hand cycles, tricycles, or supported recumbent bikes are good options.
- **Yoga and Tai Chi** — Modified poses focus on breath, balance, and gentle movement.
- **Ball exercises** — Throwing and catching a soft ball, or sitting on a therapy ball, helps coordination and core strength.

### Example Seated Routine (Cerebral Palsy)
1. **Warm Up (5 min):** Seated arm circles, ankle rotations, deep breathing
2. **Seated Marching (2 min):** Lift knees alternately while seated — focus on rhythm
3. **Arm Raises with Light Band (2 x 10 reps):** Hold a light resistance band, raise arms overhead slowly
4. **Torso Rotation (2 x 10 reps each side):** Sit tall, slowly rotate your upper body left and right
5. **Hip Flexor Stretch (2 x 20 seconds each side):** Sit at the edge of a chair, extend one leg back gently
6. **Cool Down (5 min):** Deep breathing, gentle neck stretches, positive self-talk

### Tips for Cerebral Palsy Exercise
- **Physiotherapist involvement** is strongly recommended for creating a safe routine.
- **Assistive devices** (wheelchairs, walkers, braces) should be incorporated into the exercise plan, not removed.
- Focus on **what the body CAN do**, not what it cannot.
- **Fatigue management** is important — CP requires more energy for movement. Keep sessions short initially.
- Monitor **spasticity levels** — exercises that help reduce spasticity (e.g., warm baths before exercise, gentle stretching) can make sessions more effective.

## Universal Beginner Exercise Routine

If you are not sure where to start, this routine is safe for most people:

| Exercise | Duration / Reps | Notes |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Seated March | 2 minutes | Lift knees one at a time while sitting |
| Arm Circles | 10 forward, 10 backward | Slow and controlled |
| Calf Raises | 2 x 10 reps | Stand or sit, rise up on toes |
| Seated Twist | 2 x 10 each side | Gentle rotation, breathe out as you twist |
| Deep Breathing | 5 breaths | Breathe in for 4 counts, out for 6 counts |
| Hamstring Stretch | 2 x 20 seconds each leg | Sit, extend one leg, lean forward gently |

**Frequency:** Start with 2–3 days per week. Rest on other days.
