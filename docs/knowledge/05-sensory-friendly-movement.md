# Sensory-Friendly Movement

Many neurodivergent individuals have sensory processing differences — meaning the brain processes sensory information (sound, touch, light, smell, movement) differently than expected. This can affect how exercise feels and which environments are comfortable.

This guide helps users and caregivers design sensory-friendly exercise experiences.

---

## Understanding Sensory Processing Differences

### Sensory Over-Responsivity (Hypersensitivity)
The person is MORE sensitive than average to sensory input. Small amounts of sensation feel very intense.

**Examples in exercise:**
- Loud music at the gym feels painful or overwhelming
- The texture of exercise equipment feels unpleasant on the hands
- Bright gym lighting causes headaches or discomfort
- Sweat or the feel of certain fabrics feels distressing
- Being in a crowded space causes anxiety

### Sensory Under-Responsivity (Hyposensitivity)
The person is LESS sensitive than average and may seek out more sensory input.

**Examples in exercise:**
- Craving intense physical activities — jumping, spinning, heavy lifting
- Not noticing when they are tired or in pain (important safety consideration)
- Seeking proprioceptive input (deep pressure, heavy work)
- Enjoying vigorous, high-input activities like trampolining or contact sports

### Sensory Seeking
The person actively looks for specific types of sensory input. This is common and can be incorporated positively into exercise.

## Designing a Sensory-Friendly Workout Environment

### Sound
- Use **noise-cancelling headphones** or earplugs during loud exercises
- Choose **quiet exercise spaces** — outdoor parks, home workouts, or quiet gym times
- Play **calming music** at a comfortable volume during exercise
- Avoid peak gym hours when the environment is busier and louder

### Lighting
- Prefer **natural or soft lighting** over harsh fluorescent lights
- **Dim the lights** for calming activities like yoga or stretching
- Use a **lamp instead of overhead lights** for home workouts

### Touch and Texture
- Wear **comfortable, soft clothing** — avoid scratchy seams or tight elastic
- Use **gloves** if equipment texture is uncomfortable
- Provide a **personal mat** or towel so the person always has a familiar surface
- Allow use of **weighted vests or compression garments** for proprioceptive input

### Temperature
- Keep the exercise space at a **comfortable temperature** — not too hot, not too cold
- Have **cold water** readily available, especially for individuals who overheat quickly
- Allow cooling breaks and use of a **fan or cool towel** as needed

### Crowd and Social Space
- Start with **one-on-one or small group** exercise before progressing to larger groups
- Agree on a **quiet signal** (e.g., raising a hand) meaning "I need a break"
- Provide a **designated quiet zone** the person can move to if overstimulated

## Exercises That Provide Positive Sensory Input

Some exercises are particularly effective at regulating the sensory system. These are useful both as exercise AND as calming strategies.

### Proprioceptive Activities (Body Awareness Input)
These exercises provide deep pressure and help the body feel grounded and organised:
- **Heavy work** — carrying grocery bags, pushing a weighted trolley, pulling a resistance band
- **Wall push-ups** — pressing hands firmly against a wall
- **Resistance band exercises** — the resistance provides strong proprioceptive feedback
- **Weighted exercises** — using ankle or wrist weights
- **Climbing** — on a play structure or with supervision on a rock climbing wall

### Vestibular Activities (Movement and Balance Input)
These activities stimulate the balance system and can be calming or alerting:
- **Rocking** — on a rocking chair or therapy ball
- **Swinging** — on a swing set (many adults with autism find this highly regulating)
- **Rolling on a mat** — gentle rolling movements
- **Balance board activities** — supervised
- **Swimming** — the water provides vestibular and proprioceptive input simultaneously

### Tactile Activities (Touch Input)
- **Clay or putty exercises** — squeezing and shaping activates hand muscles
- **Sand walking** — walking on different surfaces provides tactile variety
- **Water play exercise** — splashing, moving through water

## Sensory Breaks During Exercise

A **sensory break** is a planned pause to allow the nervous system to reset. Caregivers should plan these into every exercise session.

### Signs That a Sensory Break is Needed
- Covering ears or eyes
- Increased stimming (rocking, hand-flapping)
- Becoming very quiet or very agitated
- Refusing to continue the activity
- Signs of emotional distress

### What to Do During a Sensory Break
1. Move to a **quiet, low-stimulation area**
2. Offer a **familiar sensory comfort item** (fidget toy, weighted blanket, headphones)
3. Try **deep breathing** — see the Mental Wellness guide
4. Allow **5–10 minutes** before returning to exercise — do not rush
5. Offer **water and a light snack** if needed
6. After the break, offer a **gentler version** of the activity or a different activity entirely

## Pre-Exercise Sensory Preparation (Sensory Diet)

A **sensory diet** is a personalised plan of sensory activities done throughout the day to help the nervous system stay regulated. Before exercise, these activities can help:

### Alerting Activities (for low energy / under-responsive days)
- Fast walking or marching on the spot
- Jumping on a mini-trampoline (with supervision)
- Cold water on face or wrists
- Crunchy snacks
- Upbeat music

### Calming Activities (for high anxiety / over-responsive days)
- Slow, deep breathing (see breathing exercises guide)
- Gentle swinging
- Listening to soft, familiar music
- A warm drink (herbal tea, warm water)
- Light deep pressure massage (if tolerated)
- A short walk in a quiet environment

## Sample Sensory-Friendly Workout (30 Minutes)

This session is designed for someone with sensory sensitivities in a quiet home environment:

| Time | Activity | Sensory Consideration |
|------|----------|----------------------|
| 0–5 min | Sensory preparation — deep breathing + gentle rocking | Calms the nervous system |
| 5–10 min | Slow walk (indoor or outdoor, quiet route) | Proprioceptive, low stimulation |
| 10–15 min | Wall push-ups (2 x 10 reps) | Deep proprioceptive input |
| 15–20 min | Resistance band arm rows (2 x 10 reps) | Proprioceptive, predictable movement |
| 20–25 min | Seated leg raises (2 x 10 reps each leg) | Core, low sensory demand |
| 25–30 min | Cool down — seated stretching + belly breathing | Calming, predictable, no new input |

**Environment settings:** Lights dimmed, music (if used) at low volume and familiar, phone on silent, caregiver present but not intrusive.
