# Nutrition and Recovery

Good food, enough water, and good sleep all support exercise. This guide explains nutrition and recovery in plain, easy-to-understand language.

---

## Hydration (Drinking Enough Water)

**Water is the most important thing your body needs during exercise.**

- Drink **1–2 glasses of water (250–500ml)** before exercise
- Sip water **during** exercise — a small sip every 10–15 minutes
- Drink **1–2 glasses of water** after exercise to replace what you lost through sweat

### Signs You Are Not Drinking Enough Water
- Dark yellow urine (it should be pale yellow)
- Feeling tired or getting headaches
- Dry mouth
- Feeling dizzy during exercise

### Drinks to Avoid Before and During Exercise
- **Sugary drinks and soft drinks** — can cause a quick energy burst followed by a crash
- **Energy drinks** — contain high caffeine, which can cause heart racing and anxiety
- **Large amounts of juice** — high in sugar

**Plain water is always the best choice.** Coconut water is also a good option if available.

---

## Eating Before Exercise

Food gives the body energy to exercise. Here are simple guidelines:

### Timing
- **1–2 hours before exercise:** Have a light meal or snack
- **Less than 30 minutes before exercise:** Only a very small snack or nothing — eating too close to exercise can cause stomach discomfort

### Good Pre-Exercise Snacks (Light and Easy to Digest)
- A banana
- Toast with a thin spread of peanut butter
- A small bowl of rice with vegetables
- A handful of crackers
- A small yoghurt

### Foods to Avoid Just Before Exercise
- Heavy, fatty, or fried foods (take too long to digest)
- Very spicy food
- Large meals — they make you feel heavy and uncomfortable

---

## Eating After Exercise

After exercise, the body needs to **refuel** and **repair muscles**. Try to eat within **1–2 hours** after exercise.

### Good Post-Exercise Foods
- **Protein** (helps muscles recover): Eggs, chicken, fish, beans, tofu, yoghurt
- **Carbohydrates** (replaces energy): Rice, sweet potato, bread, banana, oats
- **Vegetables**: Any vegetables are good — aim for colour variety

### Simple Post-Exercise Meal Ideas
- Rice with grilled chicken and vegetables
- Egg on toast
- Banana smoothie with milk or yoghurt
- Noodle soup with vegetables and tofu

## Nutrition for Specific Conditions

### Down Syndrome
People with Down syndrome may have a **slower metabolism** (the body burns calories more slowly) and are at higher risk for obesity and thyroid problems.

- Focus on **portion control** — not strict dieting, but balanced meals
- Emphasise **vegetables, fruits, and lean proteins**
- Limit processed foods, sweet drinks, and high-sugar snacks
- **Iodine and thyroid support**: Include iodine-rich foods like seafood, eggs, and dairy if recommended by a doctor
- **Calcium and bone health**: Include dairy, tofu, leafy greens, or fortified foods for bone strength

### Cerebral Palsy
People with cerebral palsy may have difficulty chewing, swallowing, or self-feeding. This affects nutrition.

- If swallowing is difficult, consult a **speech-language pathologist** for safe food textures
- **Higher calorie needs**: Some individuals with CP have high energy expenditure due to spasticity — they may need more calories
- **Constipation** is common — increase fibre (vegetables, fruits, whole grains) and water intake
- **Iron and B12**: May be at risk of deficiency — discuss supplementation with a doctor
- Food textures may need to be **softened or blended** based on individual needs

### Autism
Many autistic individuals have **selective eating** — strong preferences or aversions to certain foods based on texture, colour, or taste. This is normal.

- **Do not force food** — this can worsen anxiety around eating
- Introduce new foods **gradually and without pressure**
- Focus on **ensuring nutritional balance** within accepted foods
- Use a **visual food schedule** if routine around mealtimes is helpful
- Discuss **supplements** (iron, Vitamin D, Omega-3) with a doctor if diet is very restricted

## Sleep and Recovery

**Sleep is when the body repairs itself.** Exercise without enough sleep leads to fatigue and slower progress.

### Recommended Sleep for Adults
- **7–9 hours per night** for most adults
- Neurodivergent individuals may need **consistent sleep schedules** to regulate the body clock

### Tips for Better Sleep
- Go to bed and wake up at the **same time every day** (even on weekends)
- Avoid screens (phones, tablets, TV) for **1 hour before bed**
- Keep the bedroom **dark, cool, and quiet** (or use white noise if helpful)
- Avoid caffeine after **2pm**
- A **short light stretching routine** or breathing exercise at bedtime can help

### Rest Days
Rest days are a **required part of any exercise program** — not a failure.

- Muscles repair and grow during rest, not during exercise
- Aim for **at least 1–2 rest days per week**
- On rest days: light walking, gentle stretching, or simply relaxing is ideal
- If you feel unusually tired, sore, or unwell — take an extra rest day

## Quick Reference: Daily Wellness Habits

| Habit | Recommendation |
|-------|---------------|
| Water intake | 6–8 glasses per day (more on exercise days) |
| Meals | 3 balanced meals + 1–2 small snacks |
| Sleep | 7–9 hours at consistent times |
| Exercise | 150 minutes of moderate activity per week (30 min x 5 days) |
| Rest days | At least 1–2 per week |
| Screen time before bed | Avoid for 1 hour before sleeping |
