# Module 2: Exercise for Neurodivergent Users

This module explains how to exercise safely and enjoyably for people with autism, Down syndrome, and cerebral palsy. All exercises are described in plain language with condition-specific guidance and progressive routines.

---

## 2.1 General Principles for Neurodivergent Fitness

1. **Always warm up first** — 5–10 minutes of gentle movement before exercise helps the body get ready and reduces injury risk.
2. **Cool down at the end** — 5 minutes of gentle stretching after exercise helps the body relax and recover.
3. **Rest is important** — rest days are part of the programme. The body needs time to recover and get stronger.
4. **Listen to your body** — if something hurts or feels very uncomfortable, stop. Pain is a signal to rest.
5. **Start small** — even 10 minutes of movement is excellent. Build up slowly over weeks and months.
6. **Have fun** — if an exercise feels boring or unpleasant, try a different one. NuroCoach can always suggest alternatives.
7. **Remove decision fatigue** — follow a structured plan with exact exercises laid out so you do not have to decide in the moment.
8. **Progressive overload** — gradually increase duration, repetitions, or resistance over time; never increase more than one variable per week.

## 2.2 Exercises for People with Autism

People with autism may have sensory sensitivities, prefer routines, or find certain environments overwhelming. Exercise benefits for autistic adults include significant increases in well-being, social interaction, emotional regulation, and decreases in distress behaviours.

### 2.2.1 Sensory-Friendly Activities

- **Walking** — a calm, predictable activity. Try walking at the same time each day to build routine. Choose quiet paths or parks to avoid sensory overload.
- **Swimming** — deep pressure from water can feel calming and provides simultaneous proprioceptive and vestibular input. Use earplugs if sounds are overwhelming.
- **Yoga** — slow, structured movements with clear steps. Choose beginner-level videos with simple instructions. Particularly beneficial for interoception (body awareness) and emotional regulation.
- **Trampolining** — many autistic individuals enjoy the proprioceptive input from bouncing. Use a safe, enclosed trampoline. Provides vestibular and alerting sensory input.
- **Cycling** — stationary bikes are a good option for a predictable, low-sensory environment. Outdoor cycling on quiet routes is also effective.
- **Martial arts** — structured, repetitive movements with clear rules; builds body awareness, strength, and self-regulation. Practise in a low-stimulation dojo.
- **Rock climbing (indoor)** — excellent proprioceptive input, engages problem-solving, and can be done in quieter gym hours.

### 2.2.2 Tips for Exercise with Autism

- Use **visual schedules** to show what the workout will look like before starting.
- Keep **routines consistent** — same time, same place when possible.
- Offer **headphones or earplugs** if the environment is noisy.
- Allow **stimming** (self-stimulatory behaviour) before, during, and after exercise to help regulate.
- Use **countdown timers** so the person knows how long each activity lasts.
- Provide **written or visual instructions** rather than only verbal cues to accommodate different processing styles.
- Address **executive function barriers**: set out equipment in advance, use a checklist, and minimise transitions between activities.
- Celebrate every effort, not just outcomes.

### 2.2.3 Autism Exercise Prescription (Evidence-Based)

**Aerobic exercise:**
- Aim for 12-week blocks, 3 sessions per week, 45–60 minutes per session
- Activities: walking, swimming, cycling, dancing
- Assessment: track distance, time, or heart rate to monitor improvement

**Strength training:**
- 12-week blocks, 2 sessions per week, 10–20 minutes per session
- 2–4 exercises, 1–3 sets, 6–12 repetitions
- Use bodyweight, resistance bands, or light dumbbells
- Combined aerobic + strength training shows greater benefits than either alone

## 2.3 Exercises for People with Down Syndrome

People with Down syndrome often have lower muscle tone (hypotonia), flexible joints, and may be at risk for Atlanto-Axial Instability (AAI). Moderate-intensity resistance training at least 2 days per week is recommended, focusing on major muscle groups and including balance training.

### 2.3.1 Recommended Activities

- **Walking and hiking** — excellent for cardiovascular health and low-risk. Aim for 150 minutes per week total.
- **Swimming** — gentle on joints, great for building strength and cardiovascular fitness. Water supports the body and reduces injury risk.
- **Dancing** — fun, rhythmic, and great for coordination. Use simple, repetitive movements. Music-based activities align with the social strengths of many people with Down syndrome.
- **Resistance band exercises** — gentle strength training that is easy to modify and targets hypotonia directly.
- **Balance exercises** — standing on one foot, walking along a line, or using a balance board (with support) helps dynamic balance and reduces fall risk.
- **Stretching routines** — important because of joint hypermobility. Focus on controlled, gentle stretches (not extreme ranges).
- **Functional strength training** — squatting to a chair, pushing, pulling, carrying bags. These build real-world independence.

### 2.3.2 Important Safety Notes for Down Syndrome

- **Atlanto-Axial Instability (AAI):** always obtain medical clearance before starting. If AAI is present, avoid exercises that involve the neck bending forward intensely (somersaults, diving, gymnastics, high-impact contact sports, headstands).
- **Congenital heart conditions:** cardiac screening is recommended before beginning a new exercise programme; approximately 40–50% of people with Down syndrome have a heart defect.
- **Joint protection:** avoid high-impact activities (jumping on hard surfaces) if joint pain is present. Use controlled range of motion — do not push joints to their end range.
- **Thermoregulation:** people with Down syndrome may have reduced ability to regulate body temperature. Monitor for overheating; exercise in cooler environments; have cold water and fans available.
- **Thyroid function:** hypothyroidism is common and affects energy levels and metabolism; ensure regular medical check-ups.

### 2.3.3 Progressive Programme for Down Syndrome

**Beginner (Weeks 1–4):**

| Exercise | Sets x Reps / Duration | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Warm up: arm circles, shoulder shrugs, leg swings | 5 min | Hold a chair for balance |
| Walking (comfortable pace) | 15 min | Outdoors or treadmill |
| Seated resistance band rows | 2 x 8 | Sit in a chair, pull band to chest |
| Supported single-leg balance | 2 x 15 sec each side | Hold a chair |
| Seated hamstring stretch | 2 x 20 sec each leg | Gentle, do not force |
| Cool down: deep breathing | 3 min | |

Frequency: 3 days per week with rest days between sessions.

**Intermediate (Weeks 5–12):**

| Exercise | Sets x Reps / Duration | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Warm up: brisk walk + dynamic stretches | 5 min | |
| Walking or light jogging | 20 min | Increase pace slightly each week |
| Standing resistance band rows | 3 x 10 | Progress from seated to standing |
| Bodyweight squats to chair | 2 x 10 | Touch chair, stand back up |
| Single-leg balance (no support) | 2 x 20 sec each side | Progress to eyes closed if safe |
| Wall push-ups | 2 x 10 | Hands on wall, lower chest toward wall |
| Calf raises | 2 x 12 | Standing, rise on toes |
| Cool down: stretching + breathing | 5 min | |

Frequency: 3–4 days per week.

**Advanced (Weeks 13+):**

| Exercise | Sets x Reps / Duration | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Warm up: dynamic movements | 5 min | |
| Brisk walking / swimming / cycling | 25–30 min | Moderate intensity |
| Resistance band exercises (full body) | 3 x 12 | Rows, chest press, bicep curls, leg press |
| Bodyweight squats (freestanding) | 3 x 12 | No chair support |
| Step-ups on low platform | 2 x 10 each leg | 15–20 cm step |
| Plank (modified on knees if needed) | 2 x 15–20 sec | Build to full plank over time |
| Balance board or single-leg reach | 2 x 30 sec each side | Supervised |
| Cool down: full-body stretching | 5 min | |

Frequency: 4–5 days per week (include 2 strength sessions, 2–3 cardio sessions).

## 2.4 Exercises for People with Cerebral Palsy

Cerebral palsy affects movement and muscle tone differently for each person. National guidelines recommend adults with CP follow standard exercise targets: 150 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity exercise per week, plus strength training on 2 or more days per week. Strengthening exercise does **not** worsen spasticity.

### 2.4.1 Recommended Activities

- **Hydrotherapy / water exercise** — water supports body weight, making movement easier. Excellent for all CP types and GMFCS levels.
- **Seated exercise** — many strength, flexibility, and cardio exercises can be done from a chair or wheelchair. Suitable for GMFCS III–V.
- **Stretching** — spasticity (muscle stiffness) is common. Regular gentle stretching helps maintain range of motion. A warm bath or warm pack before stretching can reduce tone temporarily.
- **Adaptive cycling** — hand cycles, tricycles, or supported recumbent bikes. Rhythmic auditory stimulation (exercising to a beat) shows statistically significant functional benefits.
- **Yoga and tai chi** — modified poses focus on breath, balance, and gentle movement. Can be adapted for seated or supported positions.
- **Ball exercises** — throwing and catching a soft ball, or sitting on a therapy ball, helps coordination and core strength.
- **Walking (if ambulatory)** — with or without aids (walker, crutches). Even short distances are beneficial. Treadmill walking with support is an option.
- **Horseback riding (hippotherapy)** — the horse's movement provides rhythmic vestibular and proprioceptive input; improves trunk control and balance.

### 2.4.2 Adapting by GMFCS Level

| GMFCS Level | Description | Exercise Approach |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| I | Walks without limitations | Standard exercises with minor modifications; include balance and coordination work |
| II | Walks with limitations; may use handrails on stairs | Exercises with stability support (wall, chair); avoid uneven surfaces; include hip and ankle strengthening |
| III | Walks with assistive device (walker, crutches) | Mix of standing (supported) and seated exercises; hydrotherapy is excellent; use assistive devices during exercise |
| IV | Self-mobility with limitations; transported or uses powered wheelchair | Primarily seated and upper-body exercises; recumbent cycling or hand cycling; water-based exercise with support |
| V | Transported in wheelchair; limited voluntary movement | Assisted range-of-motion exercises; seated upper-body movements with support; sensory-rich activities; caregiver-assisted stretching |

### 2.4.3 Progressive Programme for Cerebral Palsy (Seated / Wheelchair)

**Beginner (Weeks 1–4):**

| Exercise | Sets x Reps / Duration | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Seated arm circles + ankle rotations | 5 min | Warm up |
| Seated marching | 2 min | Lift knees alternately; focus on rhythm |
| Arm raises with light band | 2 x 8 | Raise arms overhead slowly |
| Torso rotation | 2 x 8 each side | Sit tall, rotate slowly |
| Hip flexor stretch | 2 x 15 sec each side | Edge of chair, extend one leg back |
| Cool down: deep breathing + neck stretches | 5 min | |

**Intermediate (Weeks 5–12):**

| Exercise | Sets x Reps / Duration | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Warm up with music (rhythmic arm and leg movements) | 5 min | Rhythmic auditory stimulation |
| Seated marching with ankle weights (0.5 kg) | 3 min | |
| Resistance band bicep curls | 3 x 10 | |
| Resistance band chest press | 2 x 10 | |
| Seated leg extensions | 2 x 10 each leg | Add ankle weight when ready |
| Trunk side bends | 2 x 10 each side | |
| Hamstring and calf stretches | 2 x 20 sec each | |
| Cool down: progressive muscle relaxation | 5 min | Tense and release each muscle group |

**Advanced (Weeks 13+):**

| Exercise | Sets x Reps / Duration | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Warm up: seated dance to music | 5 min | |
| Hand cycling or recumbent cycling | 15–20 min | Moderate effort |
| Resistance band full-body circuit | 3 x 12 | Rows, press, curls, leg press |
| Therapy ball seated balance | 2 x 30 sec | With support person nearby |
| Medicine ball chest pass (light, 1–2 kg) | 2 x 10 | With partner |
| Full-body stretching with hold | All major groups, 30 sec each | |
| Cool down: guided breathing + positive affirmation | 5 min | |

### 2.4.4 Tips for Cerebral Palsy Exercise

- **Physiotherapist involvement** is strongly recommended for creating a safe routine, especially for GMFCS III–V.
- **Assistive devices** (wheelchairs, walkers, braces) should be incorporated into the exercise plan, not removed.
- Focus on **what the body CAN do**, not what it cannot.
- **Fatigue management** is critical — CP requires more energy for movement. Keep sessions shorter initially (15–20 minutes) and build.
- **Spasticity management**: warm baths, warm packs, or light cardio before stretching can temporarily reduce spasticity and make sessions more effective.
- **Pain rule**: exercise should be pain-free during performance. Mild muscle soreness for up to 48 hours after a new programme is normal; sharp pain is not.
- Monitor for **seizure safety** — epilepsy co-occurs in 25–45% of people with CP. Ensure a safe environment and have a support person present.

## 2.5 Universal Beginner Exercise Routine

If you are not sure where to start, this routine is safe for most people:

| Exercise | Duration / Reps | Notes |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Seated march | 2 minutes | Lift knees one at a time while sitting |
| Arm circles | 10 forward, 10 backward | Slow and controlled |
| Calf raises | 2 x 10 reps | Stand or sit, rise up on toes |
| Seated twist | 2 x 10 each side | Gentle rotation, breathe out as you twist |
| Deep breathing | 5 breaths | Breathe in for 4 counts, out for 6 counts |
| Hamstring stretch | 2 x 20 seconds each leg | Sit, extend one leg, lean forward gently |

**Frequency:** start with 2–3 days per week. Rest on other days.

## 2.6 Foundational Functional Movements

Regardless of condition, building these five movement patterns improves independence in daily life:

| Movement Pattern | What It Trains | Exercise Examples |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Squat** | Getting up from a chair, toilet, car | Chair squats, wall sits, supported squats |
| **Push** | Opening doors, getting up from the floor | Wall push-ups, seated chest press with band |
| **Pull** | Carrying bags, opening drawers | Resistance band rows, pull-downs |
| **Carry / Hold** | Carrying shopping, holding objects | Farmer's walks (light weights), bag carries |
| **Locomotion** | Walking, climbing stairs, getting around | Walking, step-ups, seated marching, cycling |

These five patterns form the backbone of real-world strength and should appear in every programme.

## 2.7 Exercise Frequency and Recovery Guide

| Experience Level | Cardio Sessions / Week | Strength Sessions / Week | Session Duration | Rest Days |
|-----------------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Beginner (Weeks 1–4) | 2–3 | 1–2 | 15–20 min | 3–4 |
| Intermediate (Weeks 5–12) | 3 | 2 | 20–30 min | 2–3 |
| Advanced (Weeks 13+) | 3–4 | 2–3 | 30–45 min | 2 |

**Progressive overload rule:** increase only one variable per week — either duration, repetitions, resistance, or complexity. Never increase by more than 10% at a time.
