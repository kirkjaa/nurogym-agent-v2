# Module 4: Caregiver and Support Worker Guide

This module is for **parents, caregivers, family members, and support workers** who assist neurodivergent individuals with their fitness and wellness activities. NuroGym is designed to be used together — by the individual and their support network.

---

## 4.1 Your Role in Neurodivergent Fitness

As a caregiver or support worker, you play a crucial role in:

- **Motivating and encouraging** — your positive presence makes a huge difference
- **Ensuring safety** — watching for signs of overexertion, pain, or distress
- **Adapting activities** — modifying exercises in the moment based on how the person is feeling
- **Building routine** — helping establish consistent exercise habits
- **Communicating with professionals** — sharing observations with doctors or physiotherapists
- **Modelling** — exercising alongside the person you support increases engagement and normalises movement
- **Documenting progress** — tracking what works, what does not, and changes over time

---

## 4.2 How to Use NuroCoach with Your Client or Family Member

NuroCoach is designed to be used together or independently:

1. **Ask questions on their behalf** — if the person you support has difficulty typing or communicating, you can ask NuroCoach questions for them
2. **Read responses aloud** — for users who benefit from verbal communication
3. **Use it to plan sessions** — ask NuroCoach to suggest a weekly exercise plan based on the individual's condition and abilities
4. **Use both English and Thai** — NuroCoach responds in either language
5. **Share information** — tell NuroCoach about the person's condition, preferences, limitations, and goals so responses are more personalised
6. **Use it for your own fitness** — caregivers need movement too

## 4.3 Communication Strategies During Exercise

Effective communication helps the exercise experience feel safe and positive.

### 4.3.1 Clear, Simple Instructions

- Say one instruction at a time: "Now lift your arms up" — not "Lift your arms up and then hold for five seconds and then bring them down slowly"
- Use **visual demonstration** — show the movement before asking them to do it
- Allow **extra processing time** — wait patiently after giving an instruction. Avoid repeating too quickly; many neurodivergent individuals need 10–15 seconds to process
- Use **consistent language** — use the same words for the same movements every time (e.g., always say "lift" rather than switching between "lift", "raise", "bring up")

### 4.3.2 Positive and Strengths-Based Language

| Instead of... | Say... |
|--------------|--------|
| "That's wrong" | "Try this way" or "Let me show you again" |
| "You can't do this" | "Let's take a rest" or "Let's try something different" |
| "You need to try harder" | "You're doing your best — that's enough" |
| "Stop doing that" (re: stimming) | Allow stimming — it is a regulation tool, not a problem |
| "Everyone else can do it" | "Your body, your pace — every movement counts" |
| "Calm down" | "I can see this is hard. Let's take a break together" |

Celebrate every attempt: "Great try!", "You moved your arm — excellent work!", "You showed up today — that matters!"

### 4.3.3 Non-Verbal Communication

Many neurodivergent individuals communicate through body language, behaviour, or sounds rather than words. Watch for:

- **Pulling away or refusing** — may mean discomfort, pain, or sensory overload. Stop and check in.
- **Increased stimming** — may indicate stress, excitement, or the need to self-regulate. Adjust the session accordingly; do not suppress stimming.
- **Facial expressions of discomfort** — grimacing, frowning, avoidance of eye contact during a movement
- **Sudden changes in mood or behaviour** — may indicate fatigue, overstimulation, or physical discomfort
- **Going very quiet or "freezing"** — may indicate a shutdown; reduce demands immediately and offer a safe space
- **Covering ears or eyes** — sensory overload; move to a lower-stimulation environment

## 4.4 Positive Behaviour Support (PBS) During Exercise

PBS is a person-centred, evidence-based approach that helps understand **why** behaviours occur rather than focusing on stopping them.

### 4.4.1 Core PBS Principles for Exercise

1. **Behaviour is communication** — if someone resists exercise, the question is "What need is unmet?" not "How do I make them comply?"
2. **Build skills, not compliance** — teach self-regulation, choice-making, and communication rather than demanding obedience
3. **Be proactive** — set up the environment and routine to prevent distress before it occurs
4. **Use reinforcement, not punishment** — reward efforts and successes; never use exercise as punishment or withhold activities as punishment

### 4.4.2 Common Exercise Behaviours and PBS Responses

| Behaviour | Possible Function | PBS Response |
|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| Refusing to start exercise | Anxiety about unpredictability; fatigue; bad past experience | Offer a visual schedule; give a choice between two activities; start with the easiest, most enjoyable activity first |
| Leaving the exercise area | Sensory overload; boredom; need for a break | Offer a sensory break; reduce stimulation; shorten the session; offer a different activity |
| Becoming agitated during exercise | Pain; frustration; overwhelm; communication difficulty | Stop the exercise; check for pain; move to a calm space; use calming breathing; validate feelings |
| Repetitive requests to stop | Fatigue; anxiety; dislike of the specific activity | Honour the request; offer a rest; ask what they would prefer to do instead |
| Self-injurious behaviour during exercise | Sensory need; extreme frustration; communication of distress | Stop immediately; ensure safety; provide deep pressure or compression if helpful; seek professional support if this is recurring |

### 4.4.3 Creating a PBS-Friendly Exercise Environment

- **Reduce environmental stressors**: minimise clutter, control noise, use comfortable lighting
- **Build predictability**: use the same routine structure, visual schedule, and sequence
- **Offer choices**: "Would you like to walk or stretch first?" gives a sense of control
- **Create a calm-down zone**: a nearby quiet space with sensory items (blanket, headphones, fidget tools)
- **Keep demands low during high-stress periods**: if the person is already stressed, offer gentle movement only — or rest

## 4.5 Recognising Signs of Overexertion

Stop the exercise if you notice any of the following:

| Sign | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| Heavy, laboured breathing | Stop. Sit down. Rest. Monitor. |
| Face going very red or very pale | Stop. Move to a cool, quiet area. Offer water. |
| Complaints of chest pain or tightness | Stop immediately. Seek medical help if it continues. |
| Dizziness or stumbling | Stop. Help them sit or lie down safely. |
| Excessive sweating or shaking | Stop. Rest. Provide water and a cool environment. |
| Uncharacteristic crying, distress, or aggression | Stop. Move to a calm environment. Provide sensory comfort. |
| Sudden change in muscle tone (very stiff or very floppy) | Stop. Rest. Contact their physiotherapist if this persists. |
| Not responding to instructions (possible shutdown) | Stop. Reduce demands completely. Offer quiet, safe space. Give time. |

**Note for hyposensitive individuals**: some people (especially sensory seekers) may not notice their own fatigue or pain. As a caregiver, watch for external signs even if the person says they feel fine.

## 4.6 Adapting Activities in the Moment

Even the best-planned sessions sometimes need to change.

### If the Person is More Tired Than Usual

- Reduce the number of repetitions by half
- Switch to a seated version of the exercise
- Shorten the session and end with relaxation/breathing
- Offer a rest day — this is never a failure

### If the Person is More Energised Than Expected

- Add an extra round of their favourite activity
- Extend the walking or dancing portion
- Try a new, slightly more challenging exercise
- Channel energy into functional tasks (carrying, pushing, climbing)

### If Sensory Overload is Occurring

- Move to a quieter, lower-stimulation environment
- Offer headphones with calming music or silence
- Reduce exercise intensity — transition to slow stretching or breathing
- Allow a sensory break (fidget tool, weighted blanket, quiet time, compression vest)
- Do not rush the return to exercise — 5–15 minutes of recovery may be needed

### If the Person Refuses an Activity

- Do not force participation — ever
- Offer a choice: "Would you prefer to walk or stretch today?"
- Try a different format — music, a game, or a different location
- Take a short break and try again in 10–15 minutes
- Accept that some days, rest is the right answer

### If a Meltdown or Shutdown Occurs

- **Meltdown** (outward): ensure physical safety, remove dangerous objects, reduce stimulation, do not try to reason or give instructions. Wait. Offer comfort when the person is calmer.
- **Shutdown** (inward): reduce all demands, offer a quiet dark space, provide a blanket or comfort item, do not force communication. Wait. Check in gently when they show signs of recovery.
- After either: do not resume exercise that day. Debrief later with compassion.

## 4.7 Weekly Exercise Planning for Caregivers

A sample weekly structure that works well for many neurodivergent individuals:

| Day | Activity | Duration | Caregiver Role |
|-----|----------|----------|---------------|
| Monday | Walking (outdoors or treadmill) | 15–20 min | Walk alongside; monitor pace and fatigue |
| Tuesday | Gentle stretching or yoga | 15 min | Demonstrate movements; provide verbal encouragement |
| Wednesday | Rest or light activity (dancing, play) | Optional | Observe mood; respect if rest is preferred |
| Thursday | Strength exercises (bands, light weights) | 15–20 min | Assist with equipment; count reps aloud |
| Friday | Swimming, cycling, or favourite activity | 20–30 min | Supervise safety; focus on enjoyment |
| Saturday | Community or group activity | Flexible | Facilitate social interaction; manage sensory environment |
| Sunday | Rest | — | Model rest as positive; plan the coming week |

**Always adapt this to the individual's energy levels, preferences, and any advice from their healthcare team.**

## 4.8 Documenting and Tracking Progress

Keeping records helps you understand patterns, communicate with professionals, and celebrate growth.

### What to Track

| Category | What to Record |
|----------|---------------|
| **Activity** | What exercise was done, duration, intensity |
| **Mood** | Mood before and after (use a 1–5 scale or emoji faces) |
| **Engagement** | Level of participation (full, partial, refused), enjoyment level |
| **Physical signs** | Any pain, fatigue, overheating, spasticity changes |
| **Sensory** | Any sensory challenges, breaks needed, environmental adjustments made |
| **Behaviour** | Any notable behaviours and what may have triggered them |
| **Adaptations** | What modifications were made and whether they helped |

A simple spreadsheet, notebook, or even a voice memo after each session is sufficient. Share these records with the healthcare team at appointments.

## 4.9 Talking to the Healthcare Team

As a caregiver, you are an important link between the individual and their healthcare providers:

- Share **what exercises you have been doing** and how the person responded
- Report **any pain, unusual fatigue, or behaviour changes** during or after exercise
- Ask about **any contraindications** (exercises to avoid) specific to the individual
- Request a **formal exercise assessment** if the person has complex physical needs
- Ask whether any **medications** affect exercise capacity (some medications cause fatigue, dizziness, or heart rate changes)
- Bring your **tracking records** to appointments

NuroCoach can help you prepare questions for healthcare appointments. Just ask: "What questions should I ask my physiotherapist about [person's name]'s exercise programme?"

---

## 4.10 Self-Care for Caregivers

Caring for a neurodivergent individual is rewarding but can also be exhausting. Remember to:

- Take your own rest and exercise breaks — you cannot pour from an empty cup
- Seek support from other caregivers and support networks
- Use NuroCoach for your own fitness questions too
- Recognise caregiver burnout: persistent exhaustion, irritability, withdrawal, and feeling overwhelmed are signs to seek support
- Set boundaries: you are a vital support, not an endless resource
- Celebrate your role — the consistent support you provide makes a real difference in the person's health and happiness
