# Module 7: WHO Disability Physical Activity and Sedentary Behaviour (2020)

This module summarises the **World Health Organization’s first global guidelines on physical activity and sedentary behaviour for people living with disability** (published 2020). It aligns NuroGym’s “move at your pace” approach with international public-health targets. NuroCoach is **not** a substitute for a doctor or therapist; use this as general education.

---

## 7.1 Why These Guidelines Matter for NuroGym

- People with disabilities often face **barriers** to sport, gyms, and community programmes. The WHO states that **every type of movement counts** toward health when matched to the person’s ability.
- The guidelines were built from **systematic reviews** and support a **human rights** approach to inclusion (aligned with the WHO Global Action Plan on Physical Activity 2018–2030).

**Plain language:** If you can move safely, regular activity is one of the best things you can do for heart, mood, bones, and energy — even in small amounts.

---

## 7.2 Recommended Activity Levels (Adults With Disability)

For **adults living with disability**, WHO recommends:

| Type | Target (per week) | Plain-language examples |
|------|-------------------|-------------------------|
| **Moderate-intensity aerobic** | **150–300 minutes** | Brisk walking, cycling at a pace where you can talk but not sing, swimming, dancing |
| **OR vigorous-intensity aerobic** | **75–150 minutes** | Faster cycling, jogging (if appropriate), vigorous swimming — if cleared by a clinician |
| **OR equivalent mix** | Combine moderate and vigorous | e.g. 75 min moderate + 40 min vigorous |
| **Muscle-strengthening** | **On 2 or more days** | Resistance bands, light weights, bodyweight exercises, adapted machines |
| **Multicomponent** | **Balance and functional training** (especially older adults or fall risk) | Standing balance, sit-to-stand, tai chi–style moves — adapted to ability |
| **Sedentary behaviour** | **Reduce long sitting** | Break up long screen or sitting time with short movement breaks |

**How to start:** If 150 minutes feels impossible, **start lower**. Shorter sessions spread across the week still help. Build up over months, not days.

---

## 7.3 Safety Message From WHO

- For people **not used to activity**, begin with **light intensity** and increase gradually.
- People with **specific conditions** (heart disease, epilepsy, severe mobility limits) should get **individual advice** from a qualified professional before vigorous activity.
- **Benefits generally outweigh risks** when activity matches current health and function.

NuroCoach always reinforces: **stop** if you feel sharp pain, chest pain, severe breathlessness, dizziness, or unusual symptoms — and seek medical help when needed.

---

## 7.4 Sedentary Behaviour (Sitting Still Too Long)

- “Being active” is not only about exercise — **sitting less** also supports health.
- Practical tips for neurodivergent users and caregivers:
  - Use **timers** or **visual schedules** for “movement breaks” every 30–60 minutes (stand, stretch, walk one room, march on the spot).
  - Pair breaks with **sensory preferences** (quiet walk, wall push-ups, water sip, favourite song).
  - For wheelchair users: **weight shifts**, **arm circles**, **propulsion laps**, or **seated marching**.

---

## 7.5 Ten Areas for Inclusive Practice (Conceptual Summary)

WHO highlights multiple pillars for making physical activity **accessible**. NuroGym maps to these ideas:

| Area (simplified) | What it means for NuroGym users |
|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Individualisation** | Programmes match ability, sensory needs, and goals — not a one-size template. |
| **Supportive environments** | Quiet spaces, predictable routines, clear instructions, caregiver partnership. |
| **Skilled professionals** | Physiotherapists, OTs, and doctors when health is complex. |
| **Participation and choice** | User chooses preferred activities where possible. |
| **Monitoring and adaptation** | Change the plan when energy, pain, or mood shifts. |
