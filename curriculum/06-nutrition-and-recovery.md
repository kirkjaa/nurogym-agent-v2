# Module 6: Nutrition and Recovery

Good food, enough water, quality sleep, and proper rest all support exercise and overall wellbeing. This module explains nutrition, hydration, sleep, and recovery in plain language with condition-specific guidance backed by recent research.

---

## 6.1 Hydration (Drinking Enough Water)

**Water is the most important thing your body needs during exercise.**

- Drink **1–2 glasses of water (250–500 ml)** before exercise
- Sip water **during** exercise — a small sip every 10–15 minutes
- Drink **1–2 glasses of water** after exercise to replace what you lost through sweat
- On non-exercise days, aim for **6–8 glasses of water** across the day

### 6.1.1 Signs You Are Not Drinking Enough Water

- Dark yellow urine (it should be pale yellow)
- Feeling tired or getting headaches
- Dry mouth or cracked lips
- Feeling dizzy during exercise
- Difficulty concentrating
- Constipation (especially relevant for cerebral palsy)

### 6.1.2 Drinks to Avoid Before and During Exercise

- **Sugary drinks and soft drinks** — cause a quick energy burst followed by a crash; promote inflammation
- **Energy drinks** — contain high caffeine, which can cause heart racing, anxiety, and sleep disruption
- **Large amounts of juice** — high in sugar and low in fibre
- **Alcohol** — dehydrates the body and impairs coordination and judgement

**Plain water is always the best choice.** Coconut water is also a good option if available (it contains natural electrolytes). For long sessions (over 60 minutes), a small pinch of salt in water helps replace sodium lost through sweat.

### 6.1.3 Hydration Tips for Neurodivergent Individuals

- Use a **visual water tracker** (mark lines on a water bottle, use a chart) to build the habit
- Set **reminders or alarms** to drink — interoception challenges may mean the person does not feel thirsty even when dehydrated
- Allow preferred cups, bottles, or straws — texture and shape matter for sensory comfort
- Track urine colour as a simple, visual hydration check

## 6.2 Eating Before Exercise

Food gives the body energy to exercise. Here are simple guidelines:

### 6.2.1 Timing

- **1–2 hours before exercise:** have a light meal or snack
- **Less than 30 minutes before exercise:** only a very small snack or nothing — eating too close to exercise can cause stomach discomfort
- **Fasted exercise** (exercising without eating) is fine for some people for short, gentle sessions — but monitor energy levels closely

### 6.2.2 Good Pre-Exercise Snacks (Light and Easy to Digest)

- A banana
- Toast with a thin spread of peanut butter or almond butter
- A small bowl of rice with vegetables
- A handful of crackers or rice cakes
- A small yoghurt
- A small smoothie (banana, milk or plant milk, a handful of oats)
- An apple with a small amount of nut butter

### 6.2.3 Foods to Avoid Just Before Exercise

- Heavy, fatty, or fried foods (take too long to digest)
- Very spicy food (can cause reflux during movement)
- Large meals — they make you feel heavy and uncomfortable
- High-fibre foods in large amounts right before exercise (may cause bloating)

## 6.3 Eating After Exercise

After exercise, the body needs to **refuel** and **repair muscles**. Try to eat within **1–2 hours** after exercise.

### 6.3.1 The Three R's of Post-Exercise Nutrition

| R | What It Means | What to Eat |
|---|--------------|-------------|
| **Refuel** | Replace the energy (carbohydrates) used during exercise | Rice, sweet potato, bread, banana, oats, pasta |
| **Repair** | Give muscles the protein they need to recover and grow stronger | Eggs, chicken, fish, beans, tofu, yoghurt, lentils, nuts |
| **Rehydrate** | Replace the water and electrolytes lost through sweat | Water, coconut water, a small pinch of salt in water |

### 6.3.2 Simple Post-Exercise Meal Ideas

- Rice with grilled chicken and vegetables
- Egg on toast with avocado
- Banana smoothie with milk (or plant milk), yoghurt, and a tablespoon of oats
- Noodle soup with vegetables and tofu
- Beans on toast
- Lentil soup with bread
- Overnight oats with fruit and nuts (prepare the night before)

### 6.3.3 Snack Ideas (If a Full Meal Is Too Much)

- A handful of nuts and a piece of fruit
- Yoghurt with berries
- A boiled egg and crackers
- Hummus with vegetable sticks or bread
- A small protein bar (choose one with low sugar)

## 6.4 The Gut-Brain Axis and Neurodivergent Health

Recent research highlights a strong connection between gut health and brain function — the **gut-brain axis**. This is especially relevant for neurodivergent individuals:

### 6.4.1 What Is the Gut-Brain Axis?

The gut and brain communicate through the vagus nerve, the immune system, and chemical messengers (neurotransmitters). Approximately **90% of the body's serotonin** (a mood-regulating chemical) is produced in the gut. A healthy gut supports:

- Better mood and reduced anxiety
- Improved focus and cognitive function
- Stronger immune function
- Better sleep quality
- Reduced inflammation

### 6.4.2 Gut Health and Autism

Many autistic individuals experience gastrointestinal (GI) symptoms — constipation, diarrhoea, bloating, and abdominal pain are significantly more common than in the general population. Research shows that nutritional supplements can influence both gut microbiota composition and behavioural symptoms in neurodevelopmental conditions.

**Gut-friendly strategies:**
- Include **probiotic foods** when tolerated: yoghurt, kefir, fermented vegetables (kimchi, sauerkraut), miso
- Include **prebiotic fibre**: bananas, onions, garlic, oats, asparagus (these feed good gut bacteria)
- Introduce new foods gradually — never force
- Keep a food-symptom diary to identify triggers
- Consult a dietitian if GI symptoms are significant

### 6.4.3 Gut Health and Down Syndrome

Dietary interventions with nutrient supplementation have been shown to reduce biomarkers associated with gut dysbiosis and fungal overgrowth in Down syndrome over a 10-month period. This suggests that nutritional management can meaningfully improve gut health.

**Key strategies:**
- Prioritise fibre-rich foods (vegetables, fruits, whole grains) — constipation is common
- Include fermented foods if tolerated
- Ensure adequate hydration (supports gut motility)
- Discuss a multi-micronutrient supplement with the doctor

## 6.5 Key Micronutrients for Neurodivergent Individuals

Research consistently identifies several nutrients as particularly important for neurodivergent populations.

### 6.5.1 Vitamin D

Vitamin D demonstrates **the most robust cross-condition benefits** among neurodivergent populations. It supports:
- Neuroimmune regulation
- Serotonin production (mood and sleep)
- Bone health (critical for Down syndrome and cerebral palsy)
- Motor and cognitive outcomes

**Sources:** sunlight (15–20 minutes daily), fortified milk, fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), eggs, mushrooms.
**Supplementation:** very common — discuss dosage with a doctor. Many neurodivergent adults are deficient, especially those with limited outdoor time.

### 6.5.2 Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Omega-3s support synaptic function, brain cell membrane stability, and anti-inflammatory processes. Research shows significant relationships between omega-3 levels and symptom severity in neurodevelopmental conditions.

**Sources:** fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), walnuts, flaxseeds, chia seeds.
**Supplementation:** fish oil or algae-based omega-3 supplements. Discuss with a doctor, especially regarding dosage.

### 6.5.3 Magnesium

Magnesium is involved in over 300 biochemical reactions including muscle relaxation, sleep regulation, and nervous system function. Magnesium levels are associated with overall symptom severity in ADHD and may support anxiety reduction.

**Sources:** dark leafy greens (spinach, kale), nuts (almonds, cashews), seeds (pumpkin seeds), dark chocolate, whole grains, bananas.
**Supplementation:** magnesium glycinate or magnesium threonate are common forms. Discuss with a doctor.

### 6.5.4 Zinc

Zinc supports immune function, neurotransmitter production, and gut barrier integrity. Deficiency is common in neurodivergent populations.

**Sources:** meat, shellfish, legumes (chickpeas, lentils), seeds (pumpkin seeds), nuts, whole grains.

### 6.5.5 B-Vitamins (B6, B12, Folate)

B-vitamins are essential for energy production, neurotransmitter synthesis, and one-carbon metabolism (a cellular process critical for brain health).

**Sources:** meat, fish, eggs, dairy, leafy greens, legumes, fortified cereals.
**Note for cerebral palsy:** B12 and iron deficiency may be more common; discuss monitoring with a doctor.

### 6.5.6 Iron

Iron is essential for oxygen transport, energy, and brain function. Deficiency causes fatigue, poor concentration, and reduced exercise capacity.

**Sources:** red meat, dark leafy greens, lentils, fortified cereals, tofu.
**Note:** iron absorption is improved by pairing with vitamin C (e.g., lentils with lemon juice).

**Always discuss supplementation with a doctor or dietitian before starting. More is not always better — some nutrients can be harmful in excess.**

## 6.6 Nutrition for Specific Conditions

### 6.6.1 Autism and Selective Eating

Many autistic individuals have **selective eating** — strong preferences or aversions to certain foods based on texture, colour, smell, or taste. This is a sensory experience, not "fussiness".

**Strategies:**
- **Do not force food** — this can worsen anxiety around eating and create long-term aversions
- Introduce new foods **gradually and without pressure** — place it on the plate with no expectation to eat it; repeated exposure (up to 15–20 times) increases acceptance
- Focus on **ensuring nutritional balance** within accepted foods — a dietitian can help identify gaps
- Use a **visual food schedule** if routine around mealtimes is helpful
- Honour **food preferences** for texture — if only smooth foods are accepted, smoothies, soups, and blended meals can deliver nutrition
- Consider **supplements** (vitamin D, omega-3, iron, zinc, B-vitamins) if the diet is very restricted — always with medical guidance
- Keep a **food diary** to track intake and identify patterns

### 6.6.2 Down Syndrome and Metabolism

People with Down syndrome may have a **slower metabolism** (basal metabolic rate can be 10–15% lower than average) and are at higher risk for obesity, thyroid conditions, and coeliac disease.

**Strategies:**
- Focus on **nutrient-dense foods** — not calorie restriction, but balanced, high-quality meals
- Emphasise **vegetables, fruits, lean proteins, and whole grains**
- Limit processed foods, sugary drinks, and high-sugar snacks
- **Thyroid support**: hypothyroidism is common; regular thyroid function tests; include iodine-rich foods (seafood, eggs, dairy) if recommended
- **Calcium and bone health**: include dairy, tofu, leafy greens, or fortified foods — bone density can be lower
- **Coeliac screening**: coeliac disease is more common in Down syndrome; if GI symptoms persist, request testing
- **Portion awareness**: use visual guides (hand-sized portions) rather than calorie counting
- Support **independent eating skills** when possible — adapted cutlery can help

### 6.6.3 Cerebral Palsy and Nutrition Challenges

People with cerebral palsy may have difficulty chewing, swallowing (dysphagia), or self-feeding. This directly affects nutrition intake.

**Strategies:**
- If swallowing is difficult, consult a **speech-language pathologist** for safe food textures (the International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative — IDDSI — provides texture levels)
- **Higher calorie needs**: some individuals with CP have high energy expenditure due to spasticity and involuntary movements — they may actually need more calories, not fewer
- **Constipation** is very common — increase fibre (vegetables, fruits, whole grains), water intake, and gentle movement
- **Iron and B12**: may be at risk of deficiency — discuss monitoring and supplementation
- **Calcium and vitamin D**: essential for bone health, especially for non-ambulatory individuals with reduced weight-bearing
- Food textures may need to be **softened, chopped, or blended** based on individual needs
- Use **adaptive utensils** (weighted, angled, built-up handles) to support independence
- Position the person **upright during and after eating** (at least 20–30 minutes) to reduce reflux and aspiration risk

## 6.7 Sleep and Recovery

**Sleep is when the body repairs itself.** Exercise without enough sleep leads to fatigue, slower progress, weakened immune function, and increased emotional dysregulation.

### 6.7.1 Why Sleep Is Extra Important for Neurodivergent Individuals

Sleep disorders are significantly more common in neurodivergent populations:
- Up to **75% of people with neurodevelopmental conditions** experience insomnia, compared to 3–36% in the general population
- Sleep problems are linked to increased behavioural challenges, reduced attention, poorer emotional regulation, and lower quality of life
- Poor sleep worsens sensory sensitivities, pain perception, and spasticity

### 6.7.2 Recommended Sleep

- **7–9 hours per night** for most adults
- Neurodivergent individuals often benefit from **consistent sleep schedules** — going to bed and waking up at the same time every day, including weekends
- Some individuals may need slightly more sleep (9–10 hours) depending on activity level and condition

### 6.7.3 Evidence-Based Sleep Hygiene

Sleep hygiene (healthy sleep habits) should be the **first-line intervention** before any medication.

| Strategy | How to Do It | Why It Works |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Consistent schedule** | Same bedtime and wake time every day, including weekends | Regulates the circadian rhythm |
| **Wind-down routine** | 30–60 min before bed: dim lights, quiet activities, gentle stretching | Signals the brain to prepare for sleep |
| **Screen curfew** | Avoid screens (phone, tablet, TV) for 1 hour before bed | Blue light suppresses melatonin production |
| **Dark, cool, quiet room** | Blackout curtains, 16–19°C, earplugs or white noise machine | Reduces sensory input that can prevent sleep |
| **Limit caffeine** | No caffeine after 2 pm (ideally noon) | Caffeine has a half-life of 5–6 hours |
| **Exercise timing** | Finish exercise at least 2–3 hours before bed | Exercise raises body temperature and adrenaline; the body needs time to cool down |
| **Bedtime stretching or breathing** | 5 min of gentle stretching + 4-7-8 breathing | Activates parasympathetic nervous system |
| **Avoid large meals before bed** | Finish eating 2–3 hours before sleep | Digestion can disrupt sleep quality |
| **Weighted blanket** | Use if the individual finds deep pressure calming | Provides proprioceptive input that reduces anxiety and promotes sleep |

### 6.7.4 When Sleep Hygiene Is Not Enough — Melatonin

If behavioural and sleep hygiene strategies are unsuccessful after consistent effort (4–6 weeks), melatonin may be discussed with a doctor.

- Melatonin is a hormone the body naturally produces to regulate the sleep-wake cycle
- Low-dose melatonin (e.g., 1–3 mg at bedtime) can shorten the time it takes to fall asleep by approximately 40–45 minutes and extend total sleep by 20–25 minutes
- It should always be used **alongside** good sleep hygiene, not as a replacement
- **Important**: before starting melatonin, underlying sleep disorders (obstructive sleep apnoea, periodic limb movement disorder) should be assessed, as these are common in cerebral palsy and Down syndrome
- Long-term safety data is still limited; regular review with a doctor is recommended
- Never start melatonin without medical guidance

### 6.7.5 Condition-Specific Sleep Notes

**Autism:** sleep onset insomnia (difficulty falling asleep) is very common; melatonin production may be disrupted; weighted blankets and consistent routines are often effective; screen-based special interests before bed can delay sleep significantly.

**Down syndrome:** obstructive sleep apnoea is common due to airway anatomy; snoring, gasping, or restless sleep should be assessed by a sleep specialist; proper positioning during sleep can help.

**Cerebral palsy:** pain, spasticity, and positioning difficulties can disrupt sleep; a physiotherapist or occupational therapist can advise on sleep positioning systems; some medications for spasticity may aid or hinder sleep.

## 6.8 Rest Days and Active Recovery

Rest days are a **required part of any exercise programme** — not a failure.

### 6.8.1 Why Rest Matters

- Muscles repair and grow stronger during rest, not during exercise
- The nervous system recovers and consolidates motor learning
- Chronic exercise without rest leads to overtraining: fatigue, mood changes, increased injury risk, reduced immune function

### 6.8.2 Rest Day Guidelines

- Aim for **at least 1–2 rest days per week** (beginners may need 3–4)
- On rest days: light walking, gentle stretching, or simply relaxing is ideal
- If you feel unusually tired, sore, or unwell — take an extra rest day without guilt
- Rest is especially important after introducing new exercises or increasing intensity

### 6.8.3 Active Recovery Options

Active recovery is very gentle movement that promotes blood flow without adding training stress:

- Slow walk (15–20 min at a comfortable pace)
- Gentle yoga or stretching (15 min)
- Swimming at an easy pace
- Foam rolling or self-massage (if tolerated)
- Light household tasks (gardening, tidying)
- Play (games, ball toss, dancing gently)

## 6.9 Quick Reference: Daily Wellness Habits

| Habit | Recommendation |
|-------|---------------|
| Water intake | 6–8 glasses per day (more on exercise days) |
| Meals | 3 balanced meals + 1–2 small snacks |
| Protein | Include at every meal to support muscle recovery |
| Fruits and vegetables | Aim for 5+ servings daily; colour variety |
| Sleep | 7–9 hours at consistent times |
| Exercise | 150 minutes of moderate activity per week (30 min x 5 days) |
| Strength training | 2 sessions per week (major muscle groups) |
| Rest days | At least 1–2 per week |
| Screen time before bed | Avoid for 1 hour before sleeping |
| Supplements | Discuss vitamin D, omega-3, and magnesium with your doctor |
| Gut health | Include probiotic and prebiotic foods when tolerated |
