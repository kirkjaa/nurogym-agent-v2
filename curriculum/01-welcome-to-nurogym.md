# Module 1: Welcome to NuroGym

NuroGym is a fitness and wellness app made for people who are neurodivergent. This includes people with:
- **Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)**
- **Down Syndrome**
- **Cerebral Palsy**

NuroGym is also for **caregivers, parents, and support workers** who help neurodivergent individuals stay active and healthy.

---

## 1.1 What Is NuroCoach?

NuroCoach is your personal AI fitness companion inside NuroGym. NuroCoach can:

- Suggest fun and safe exercises that match your abilities
- Give step-by-step instructions in simple language
- Help you feel motivated and supported
- Answer questions in **English and Thai (ภาษาไทย)**
- Remember safety rules specific to your condition
- Search for the latest fitness and wellness research when needed
- Remind you that rest, fun, and going at your own pace are all part of the journey

## 1.2 Why Fitness Matters for Neurodivergent Individuals

People with disabilities are **three times more likely** to develop chronic health problems if they are not physically active (WHO, 2023). Adults with intellectual and developmental disabilities who participate in adapted fitness programmes show significant improvements in BMI, balance, and muscle strength over 12 months of regular activity.

### Physical benefits

- **Cardiovascular health** — reduces risk of heart disease and type 2 diabetes
- **Bone density** — weight-bearing exercise strengthens bones, especially important for people with Down syndrome and cerebral palsy who may have lower bone density
- **Motor skills** — practice improves coordination, balance, and functional mobility over time
- **Pain management** — gentle movement can reduce chronic pain and spasticity-related discomfort in cerebral palsy
- **Weight management** — regular activity helps maintain a healthy weight, especially important given the slower metabolism common in Down syndrome

### Mental and emotional benefits

- **Mood** — exercise releases endorphins (natural feel-good chemicals) and reduces symptoms of depression and anxiety
- **Sleep** — physical activity improves sleep quality and helps regulate the body clock
- **Focus and attention** — movement helps the brain stay alert and calm; the prefrontal cortex functions more effectively after exercise
- **Self-confidence** — achieving movement goals builds a positive self-image
- **Emotional regulation** — physical activity helps the nervous system move between states of arousal more smoothly

### Social benefits

- **Social connection** — group activities and programmes build friendships and reduce isolation
- **Communication practice** — structured exercise with a partner or caregiver provides opportunities for verbal and non-verbal communication
- **Community belonging** — participating in fitness communities creates a sense of identity beyond a diagnosis

## 1.3 Understanding Each Condition

### Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Autism is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by differences in social communication, sensory processing, and behavioural patterns. Key considerations for fitness:

- **Sensory processing differences** — sounds, textures, lighting, and crowds in exercise environments may be overwhelming (hypersensitivity) or under-stimulating (hyposensitivity)
- **Preference for routine and predictability** — sudden changes in exercise plans may cause anxiety
- **Executive function challenges** — difficulties with planning, task initiation, and transitioning between activities; up to 70% of autistic people experience executive function difficulties
- **Motor coordination** — some autistic individuals have differences in motor planning and coordination
- **Alexithymia** — difficulty identifying and describing emotions, which affects recognising fatigue or pain signals
- **Strengths** — strong pattern recognition, deep focus on interests, honesty, and determination

### Down Syndrome

Down syndrome is a genetic condition caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21. Key considerations for fitness:

- **Hypotonia (low muscle tone)** — muscles may feel looser and tire more quickly; resistance training is especially beneficial
- **Joint hypermobility (ligamentous laxity)** — joints are more flexible than typical, increasing injury risk; controlled movements are essential
- **Atlanto-Axial Instability (AAI)** — approximately 10–30% of people with Down syndrome have instability of the top two vertebrae of the neck; medical screening is required before starting exercise; somersaults, diving, gymnastics, and high-impact contact sports must be avoided if AAI is present
- **Congenital heart conditions** — approximately 40–50% of people with Down syndrome are born with a heart defect; cardiac screening is recommended before exercise programmes begin
- **Slower metabolism** — higher risk of obesity and thyroid conditions; nutrition guidance is important alongside fitness
- **Thermoregulation** — reduced ability to regulate body temperature during exercise; overheating risk is higher
- **Strengths** — social nature, enjoyment of music and rhythm, enthusiasm for group activities, strong visual learning

### Cerebral Palsy (CP)

Cerebral palsy is a group of conditions affecting movement and posture caused by damage to the developing brain. Key considerations for fitness:

- **Spasticity** — increased muscle stiffness and tightness; gentle stretching and warm-up are essential; importantly, strengthening exercise does **not** worsen spasticity
- **GMFCS levels** — the Gross Motor Function Classification System (Levels I–V) describes functional mobility; exercise programmes should be adapted to the individual's GMFCS level
- **Fatigue** — CP requires more energy for movement; sessions should be shorter and rest periods longer
- **Pain** — chronic musculoskeletal pain is common; exercise should always be pain-free during performance
- **Dysarthria and communication** — some individuals have difficulty with speech; non-verbal communication and assistive technology should be accommodated
- **Seizure risk** — epilepsy co-occurs in approximately 25–45% of people with CP; supervised exercise and seizure-safe environments are important
- **Strengths** — resilience, adaptability, determination, and strong bonds with support networks

## 1.4 NuroGym's Promises

1. **Safety first** — every exercise suggestion is designed to be safe. If something feels wrong, stop and rest.
2. **Plain language always** — we explain every fitness term in words that are easy to understand.
3. **Your pace, your rules** — there is no "normal" speed. Every step forward — big or small — is a win.
4. **Evidence-informed** — our guidance is based on current research and best-practice guidelines for neurodivergent fitness.
5. **Inclusive** — NuroGym respects every body, every ability level, and every way of communicating.

## 1.6 Who Can Use NuroGym?

NuroGym is designed for individuals aged **24–50** who are neurodivergent, but it is welcoming to anyone. Caregivers and support workers are especially encouraged to use it alongside the individuals they support.

---

## 1.7 A Note on Medical Advice

NuroCoach provides **general wellness and fitness guidance**. It is not a replacement for medical advice.

Before starting any new exercise programme, please check with:
- Your **doctor** — especially for cardiac screening (Down syndrome), seizure management (CP, epilepsy), and AAI screening (Down syndrome)
- Your **physiotherapist** — for personalised movement assessments and spasticity management
- Your **occupational therapist** — for sensory processing assessments and assistive device recommendations

This is especially important if you have a heart condition, epilepsy, bone or joint problems, or any other medical condition that affects exercise.
