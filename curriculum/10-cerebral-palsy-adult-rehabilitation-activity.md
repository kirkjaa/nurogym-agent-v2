# Module 10: Cerebral Palsy — Adult Activity, Rehabilitation, and Pain

This module summarises **evidence-informed themes** for **adults with cerebral palsy (CP)**: activity targets, **strength and spasticity**, **pain and fatigue**, **falls**, and **how rehabilitation fits** with community fitness. CP is **heterogeneous** — always individualise.

---

## 10.1 Adults With CP Are Active Participants in Health

- Adults with CP benefit from the **same public-health idea** as other adults: **regular physical activity** supports **heart health**, **mood**, **bone density**, **metabolic health**, and **participation** in daily life.
- National hospital guidance (e.g. UK NHS trust materials) often states adults with CP can follow **general adult activity recommendations** **adapted** to function — **not** a separate “lesser” standard, but **tailored execution**.

---

## 10.2 Typical Activity Targets (Adapted, Not One-Size)

Commonly cited targets for **ambulatory and many non-ambulatory** adults mirror population guidelines where safe:

| Domain | Guideline theme | Adaptation for CP |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Aerobic** | ~**150 min/week moderate** (or 75 min vigorous) **or** equivalent | Use walking, hand-cycling, swimming, wheelchair propulsion, rowing — **as appropriate** |
| **Strength** | **2+ days/week** major muscle groups | Seated, supported, or machine-based; **resistance bands**; avoid pain during movement |
| **Sedentary time** | **Break up long sitting** | Weight shifts, pressure relief, arm ergometry “snacks” |

**Beginners:** shorter sessions, more rest, slower progression.

---

## 10.3 Spasticity, Strength, and Misconceptions

- **Strength training does not inherently worsen spasticity** in a harmful way when programmed appropriately — clinical sources emphasise **pain-free** loading and **gradual** progression.
- **Stretching** may help **comfort and range**; combine with **warm-up** and **positioning** advice from PT.
- **Spasticity fluctuations** — fatigue, illness, and stress can change tone; **adjust** the session rather than pushing through.

---

## 10.4 Pain, Fatigue, and Secondary Conditions

- **Pain** is common in adults with CP (musculoskeletal overuse, contractures, neuropathic components). **Exercise should not reproduce sharp pain.**  
- **Fatigue** — movement often costs **more energy**; plan **shorter** workouts and **recovery days**.  
- **Falls** — balance work and environmental safety (clear space, footwear, aids) matter.  
- **Osteoporosis / bone health** — especially if **non-ambulatory**; discuss **weight-bearing** and **vitamin D / calcium** with clinicians.

---

## 10.5 Rehabilitation Modalities Often Used Alongside Fitness

| Modality | Role |
|----------|------|
| **Physiotherapy** | Gait, stretching, strength, pain strategies, equipment recommendations |
| **Hydrotherapy** | Buoyancy reduces load; good for mobility and pain for many |
| **Orthotics / seating** | Positioning for walking and wheelchair; affects exercise choice |
| **Botulinum toxin / medical spasticity care** | Medical management — not fitness advice; coordinated with specialists |

---

## 10.6 Mental Health and Participation

- Chronic pain and barriers to access can affect **mood** and **social participation**.  
- Activity choices that feel **meaningful** (music, outdoor time, adaptive sport) support **adherence** better than generic “must exercise.”

---

## 10.7 References and Further Reading

- *Disability and Rehabilitation* / *Developmental Medicine & Child Neurology* — systematic reviews on **physical activity and exercise in adults with CP** (search PubMed).  
- NHS patient information: **Exercise and activity for adults with cerebral palsy** (e.g. UCLH-style leaflets) for **general** education.  
- APTA and national CP associations — **adult transition** and **lifelong care** resources.

## 10.8 NuroCoach Boundaries

- We do **not** prescribe medical spasticity treatment or interpret **GMFCS** without clinician input.  
- We **do** support **safe**, **graded**, **sensory-aware** movement and **caregiver** collaboration — see Modules **2**, **4**, and **5**.
