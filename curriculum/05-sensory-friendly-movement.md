# Module 5: Sensory-Friendly Movement

Many neurodivergent individuals have sensory processing differences — meaning the brain processes sensory information (sound, touch, light, smell, movement) differently than expected. This module provides a deep understanding of sensory systems, how to design sensory-friendly exercise experiences, and evidence-based strategies for sensory regulation through movement.

---

## 5.1 Understanding Sensory Processing Differences

Sensory integration is the neurological process of organising sensory information from the body and the environment to produce adaptive responses. The framework was developed by Dr A. Jean Ayres in the 1970s and is now known as Ayres Sensory Integration (ASI). Sensory processing differences are common in autism, cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, and ADHD.

### 5.1.1 The Eight Sensory Systems

Most people know five senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell), but there are three additional systems that are critical for movement and exercise:

| Sensory System | What It Does | Relevance to Exercise |
|----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Visual** | Processes light, colour, shape, movement | Gym lighting, visual clutter, watching demonstrations |
| **Auditory** | Processes sound, volume, pitch | Music, instructor voice, gym noise, echoes |
| **Tactile** | Processes touch, pressure, texture, pain, temperature | Equipment texture, clothing feel, sweat, physical contact |
| **Gustatory** | Processes taste | Food and drink choices around exercise |
| **Olfactory** | Processes smell | Gym smells, cleaning products, body odour, chlorine in pools |
| **Proprioceptive** | Senses body position, muscle tension, joint pressure | Knowing where your limbs are during exercise; feeling grounded |
| **Vestibular** | Senses balance, head position, acceleration, spinning | Balance exercises, cycling, swimming, any movement involving the head |
| **Interoceptive** | Senses internal body signals: heart rate, hunger, thirst, temperature, pain, need for toilet | Knowing when you are tired, overheated, thirsty, or in pain during exercise |

### 5.1.2 Sensory Over-Responsivity (Hypersensitivity)

The person is MORE sensitive than average to sensory input. Small amounts of sensation feel very intense.

**Examples in exercise:**
- Loud music at the gym feels painful or overwhelming
- The texture of exercise equipment (rubber grips, foam mats) feels unpleasant on the hands
- Bright fluorescent gym lighting causes headaches or visual distress
- Sweat on the skin or certain fabric textures feels distressing
- Being in a crowded space causes anxiety or panic
- The smell of a gym or pool chemicals is overwhelming
- Being touched or bumped during group exercise feels alarming

### 5.1.3 Sensory Under-Responsivity (Hyposensitivity)

The person is LESS sensitive than average and may not register sensory input that others notice easily.

**Examples in exercise:**
- Craving intense physical activities — jumping, spinning, heavy lifting, deep pressure
- Not noticing fatigue, pain, dehydration, or overheating (**critical safety concern** — caregivers must monitor)
- Seeking proprioceptive input: heavy work, tight compression, firm hugs
- Enjoying vigorous, high-input activities like trampolining, contact sports, or running
- Not noticing when clothing is twisted or equipment is positioned incorrectly

### 5.1.4 Sensory Seeking

The person actively looks for specific types of sensory input. This is common, natural, and can be incorporated positively into exercise rather than suppressed.

**Common exercise-related sensory seeking:**
- Wanting to jump, spin, bounce, or rock
- Enjoying the feeling of heavy weights or compression
- Preferring loud music during exercise
- Rubbing hands on different textures
- Seeking intense movement experiences

## 5.2 Designing a Sensory-Friendly Workout Environment

### 5.2.1 Sound

- Use **noise-cancelling headphones** or earplugs during loud exercises or in gym environments
- Choose **quiet exercise spaces** — outdoor parks, home workouts, or quiet gym times (many gyms offer "sensory hours")
- Play **calming music** at a comfortable volume, or use silence if preferred
- Avoid peak gym hours when the environment is busier and louder
- Use **consistent, predictable sounds**: a rhythmic playlist or metronome can help regulate pace
- If instructions are verbal, speak slowly, clearly, and at a moderate volume

### 5.2.2 Lighting

- Prefer **natural or soft lighting** over harsh fluorescent lights
- **Dim the lights** for calming activities like yoga or stretching
- Use a **lamp instead of overhead lights** for home workouts
- Wear **tinted glasses or a cap with a visor** if fluorescent lights cannot be avoided
- Avoid flickering lights, strobe effects, or rapidly changing visual stimuli

### 5.2.3 Touch and Texture

- Wear **comfortable, soft clothing** with flat seams, no scratchy labels, and not too tight or too loose
- Use **gloves** if equipment texture is uncomfortable (rubber, foam, metal)
- Provide a **personal mat or towel** so the person always has a familiar surface
- Allow use of **weighted vests or compression garments** for proprioceptive input
- Be aware that **sweat** can be a sensory trigger — keep towels accessible and take wipe-down breaks
- Warn before any physical contact (spotting, adjusting form)

### 5.2.4 Temperature

- Keep the exercise space at a **comfortable, consistent temperature**
- Have **cold water** readily available, especially for individuals who overheat quickly (common in Down syndrome)
- Allow cooling breaks and use of a **fan, cool towel, or misting spray** as needed
- Be aware that some people are **cold-sensitive** — ensure the space is warm enough and allow layers

### 5.2.5 Smell

- Use **unscented cleaning products** in the exercise space
- Avoid strong air fresheners or essential oil diffusers unless the person prefers them
- If exercising in a pool, check whether chlorine levels are tolerable; some people need nose clips
- Allow the person to use a **familiar scent** (a personal item, a mild essential oil on a wristband) as a grounding tool

### 5.2.6 Crowd and Social Space

- Start with **one-on-one or small group** exercise before progressing to larger groups
- Agree on a **quiet signal** (e.g., raising a hand, tapping out, a specific word) meaning "I need a break"
- Provide a **designated quiet zone** the person can move to if overstimulated
- Allow the person to position themselves away from the centre of the group
- Respect that some people prefer **solo exercise** and do not need group settings to succeed

## 5.3 Exercises That Provide Positive Sensory Input

Some exercises are particularly effective at regulating the sensory system. These are useful both as exercise AND as calming or alerting strategies.

### 5.3.1 Proprioceptive Activities (Body Awareness / Deep Pressure)

These exercises provide deep pressure and help the body feel grounded and organised. Proprioceptive input is almost always calming and regulating.

- **Heavy work** — carrying grocery bags, pushing a weighted trolley, pulling a sled or resistance band, gardening tasks
- **Wall push-ups** — pressing hands firmly against a wall (10–20 reps)
- **Resistance band exercises** — the resistance provides strong proprioceptive feedback throughout the movement
- **Weighted exercises** — using ankle weights, wrist weights, or a weighted vest during walking
- **Climbing** — on a play structure, indoor climbing wall (supervised), or cargo net
- **Bear crawls and crab walks** — weight through the arms provides deep joint input
- **Joint compressions** — a caregiver can provide gentle rhythmic compressions through the shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees, and ankles
- **Firm hugs or squeeze machine** — deep pressure can be deeply calming (always with consent)
- **Carrying a heavy backpack** — during walks, the weight provides ongoing proprioceptive input
- **Kneading dough or squeezing therapy putty** — strengthens hands while providing tactile-proprioceptive input

### 5.3.2 Vestibular Activities (Movement and Balance)

These activities stimulate the balance system and can be calming (slow, rhythmic) or alerting (fast, unpredictable):

**Calming vestibular (slow, rhythmic, linear):**
- Rocking in a rocking chair or on a therapy ball
- Gentle swinging (linear, back-and-forth)
- Rolling slowly on a mat
- Slow dancing or swaying

**Alerting vestibular (fast, rotational, unpredictable):**
- Spinning (supervised, in short bursts — stop if nausea or dizziness occur)
- Jumping on a trampoline
- Quick direction changes during walking or games
- Balance board or wobble board activities (supervised)

**Both:**
- Swimming — water provides vestibular and proprioceptive input simultaneously
- Cycling — rhythmic, with vestibular input from balance and turning
- Horseback riding (hippotherapy) — the horse's gait provides rhythmic vestibular-proprioceptive input

### 5.3.3 Tactile Activities (Touch Input)

- **Clay, putty, or stress ball** — squeezing and shaping activates hand muscles and provides tactile feedback
- **Sand walking** — walking on different surfaces (grass, sand, gravel) provides tactile variety
- **Water exercise** — splashing, moving through water, feeling resistance
- **Textured mat walking** — stepping on mats with different textures (bumpy, smooth, soft, firm)
- **Brush protocol (Wilbarger)** — a specific therapeutic brushing technique used by occupational therapists for tactile defensiveness (must be trained by an OT)

### 5.3.4 Interoceptive Activities (Internal Body Awareness)

Since many neurodivergent people have reduced interoception, exercises that build this awareness are valuable:

- **Heart rate monitoring** — check pulse before, during, and after exercise; notice the difference
- **Temperature awareness** — notice whether your face feels warm after exercise; hold a cold water bottle and notice the sensation
- **Muscle tension/release** — progressive muscle relaxation: tense a muscle group for 5 seconds, then release; notice the contrast
- **Breathing awareness** — place hands on chest and belly; notice which moves more
- **Hunger and thirst check-ins** — before and after exercise, rate hunger and thirst on a scale of 1–10

## 5.4 Sensory Breaks During Exercise

A **sensory break** is a planned pause to allow the nervous system to reset. Caregivers should plan these into every exercise session.

### 5.4.1 Signs That a Sensory Break is Needed

- Covering ears or eyes
- Increased stimming (rocking, hand-flapping, vocalising)
- Becoming very quiet, "frozen", or withdrawing
- Refusing to continue the activity
- Signs of emotional distress (tears, anger, panic)
- Clumsiness or loss of coordination (nervous system overload can affect motor skills)
- Complaints of headache, nausea, or dizziness (may indicate vestibular overload)

### 5.4.2 What to Do During a Sensory Break

1. Move to a **quiet, low-stimulation area** — fewer people, dimmer lights, less noise
2. Offer a **familiar sensory comfort item** (fidget toy, weighted blanket, headphones, favourite texture)
3. Try **deep breathing** — see Module 3 breathing exercises
4. Offer **proprioceptive input** — a firm hug (with consent), wall push-ups, joint compressions, or a weighted lap pad
5. Allow **5–15 minutes** before considering returning to exercise — do not rush
6. Offer **water and a light snack** if needed
7. After the break, offer a **gentler version** of the activity or a different activity entirely
8. If the person does not want to return to exercise, that is okay — the break itself is valid

## 5.5 Pre-Exercise Sensory Preparation (Sensory Diet)

A **sensory diet** is a personalised plan of sensory activities done throughout the day to help the nervous system stay regulated. Before exercise, specific preparation activities can set the person up for success.

### 5.5.1 Alerting Activities (for Low Energy / Under-Responsive Days)

Use these when the person seems sluggish, disconnected, or under-aroused:

- Fast walking or marching on the spot (1–2 minutes)
- Jumping on a mini-trampoline (with supervision)
- Cold water on face, wrists, or back of neck
- Crunchy snacks (carrots, crackers, apple)
- Upbeat, rhythmic music
- Clapping games or rhythmic hand exercises
- Bright, stimulating visual input (a short energising video)

### 5.5.2 Calming Activities (for High Anxiety / Over-Responsive Days)

Use these when the person seems anxious, agitated, or overstimulated:

- Slow, deep breathing (see Module 3 breathing exercises)
- Gentle rocking in a chair or on a therapy ball
- Listening to soft, familiar music or nature sounds
- A warm drink (herbal tea, warm water with lemon)
- Light deep-pressure massage on shoulders or arms (if tolerated)
- A short walk in a quiet, natural environment
- Weighted blanket or compression vest for 10–15 minutes
- Dimmed lighting and reduced conversation

### 5.5.3 Organising Activities (for Scattered / Dysregulated Days)

Use these when the person seems "all over the place" — not clearly over- or under-stimulated, but disorganised:

- Heavy work: carrying a bag of books, pushing a trolley, pulling a resistance band
- Chewy or resistive foods: dried fruit, bagel, jerky
- Oral motor activities: drinking through a straw (thick smoothie), blowing bubbles
- Structured, repetitive movements: marching with high knees, counting exercises aloud

## 5.6 Sample Sensory-Friendly Workouts

### 5.6.1 Calming Sensory Workout (30 Minutes — Hypersensitive / Anxious Day)

**Environment:** lights dimmed, phone on silent, familiar music at low volume (or silence), caregiver present but not intrusive.

| Time | Activity | Sensory Purpose |
|------|----------|----------------|
| 0–5 min | Deep breathing + gentle rocking on therapy ball | Calms nervous system; rhythmic vestibular input |
| 5–10 min | Slow walk (indoor or quiet outdoor route) | Proprioceptive and mild vestibular; low stimulation |
| 10–15 min | Wall push-ups (2 x 10 reps) | Deep proprioceptive input to ground the body |
| 15–20 min | Resistance band arm rows (2 x 10 reps) | Proprioceptive; predictable, controlled movement |
| 20–25 min | Seated leg raises (2 x 10 each leg) | Core work with low sensory demand |
| 25–30 min | Seated stretching + belly breathing | Calming; predictable; no new sensory input |

### 5.6.2 Alerting Sensory Workout (30 Minutes — Hyposensitive / Low Energy Day)

**Environment:** brighter lighting, upbeat music, more verbal encouragement, dynamic pace.

| Time | Activity | Sensory Purpose |
|------|----------|----------------|
| 0–3 min | Cold water on face + marching on spot | Alerting tactile + proprioceptive input |
| 3–8 min | Brisk walk with weighted vest or backpack | Proprioceptive loading; increased heart rate |
| 8–13 min | Jumping jacks or mini-trampoline (with support) | Strong vestibular + proprioceptive; very alerting |
| 13–18 min | Medicine ball slams or chest passes (1–2 kg) | Explosive proprioceptive; engaging |
| 18–23 min | Bear crawls or crab walks (2 x length of room) | Weight through arms; full-body proprioceptive |
| 23–28 min | Resistance band full-body circuit (fast-paced) | Sustained proprioceptive with moderate cardio |
| 28–30 min | Cool down: stretching + humming breath | Transition back to calm state |

### 5.6.3 Organising Sensory Workout (25 Minutes — Dysregulated Day)

**Environment:** structured, predictable; visual schedule displayed; timer visible; moderate stimulation.

| Time | Activity | Sensory Purpose |
|------|----------|----------------|
| 0–5 min | Heavy work: carry a bag of books across room (5 trips) | Organising proprioceptive input |
| 5–10 min | Resistance band rows + presses (alternating, 2 x 10) | Rhythmic proprioceptive; structured |
| 10–15 min | Structured walk: count steps to 100, rest, repeat x 3 | Cognitive engagement + proprioceptive; organising |
| 15–20 min | Balance board or single-leg balance (2 x 20 sec each) | Vestibular + proprioceptive integration |
| 20–25 min | Progressive muscle relaxation + deep breathing | Interoceptive awareness; calming closure |
