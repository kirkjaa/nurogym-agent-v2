# Module 3: Mental Wellness and Motivation

NuroGym believes that mental health and physical health are deeply connected. This module covers mood support, emotional regulation, breathing exercises, motivation strategies, executive function support, and when to seek professional help.

---

## 3.1 Why Mental Wellness Matters in Fitness

Exercise is not just about the body — it also affects the mind. Regular physical activity has been shown to:

- Reduce **anxiety** and feelings of worry
- Improve **mood** by releasing endorphins (natural feel-good chemicals) and increasing serotonin
- Help with **sleep quality** by regulating the circadian rhythm
- Build **self-confidence** over time
- Reduce symptoms of **depression**
- Improve **focus and attention** by increasing blood flow to the prefrontal cortex
- Improve **emotional regulation** — the ability to manage strong feelings without becoming overwhelmed

For neurodivergent individuals, these benefits can be especially powerful. Movement can serve as a form of **sensory regulation** — helping the nervous system feel calmer and more organised.

## 3.2 Understanding Mood Before Exercise

Before starting a workout, it helps to check in with how you are feeling. NuroCoach uses mood awareness to suggest the right kind of activity.

### Mood Check Guide

| How I Feel | Suggested Activity | Why It Helps |
|------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Happy and energetic | Active exercise — walking, dancing, cycling | Channel positive energy into productive movement |
| Calm and relaxed | Yoga, stretching, gentle movement | Maintain the calm state while adding gentle activity |
| Anxious or worried | Breathing exercises, slow walking, gentle yoga | Activates the parasympathetic nervous system to reduce the stress response |
| Tired or low energy | Rest or very gentle stretching — it is okay to rest | Prevents overexertion and respects the body's signals |
| Overwhelmed or overstimulated | Quiet sensory break first, then decide | Allows the nervous system to reset before adding more input |
| Sad or low mood | Gentle movement if comfortable; breathing exercises; reach out to a trusted person | Even 5 minutes of movement can shift brain chemistry |
| Frustrated or angry | Vigorous movement if safe (fast walking, punching bag, resistance band pulls) | Physical exertion helps discharge the fight-or-flight energy |
| Numb or disconnected | Proprioceptive activities (wall push-ups, carrying something heavy, compression) | Strong body-awareness input can help reconnect mind and body |

**It is always okay to rest.** There is no pressure to exercise if you are not feeling well mentally or physically.

## 3.3 Breathing Exercises

Breathing exercises are quick, effective, and can be done anywhere. They help calm the nervous system, reduce anxiety, and prepare the body for exercise. These techniques work by stimulating the **vagus nerve**, which activates the parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) nervous system.

### 3.3.1 The 4-7-8 Breathing (Calming Breath)

1. Breathe **in** through your nose for **4 counts**
2. **Hold** your breath for **7 counts**
3. Breathe **out** slowly through your mouth for **8 counts**
4. Repeat 3–4 times

**Best for:** anxiety, feeling overwhelmed, before starting exercise, before sleep.

### 3.3.2 Box Breathing (Focus Breath)

1. Breathe **in** for **4 counts**
2. **Hold** for **4 counts**
3. Breathe **out** for **4 counts**
4. **Hold** for **4 counts**
5. Repeat 4 times

**Best for:** building focus, managing stress, calming the mind, preparing for a task.

### 3.3.3 Belly Breathing (Grounding Breath)

1. Place one hand on your belly
2. Breathe **in** slowly — feel your belly rise (not your chest)
3. Breathe **out** slowly — feel your belly fall
4. Repeat 5–10 times

**Best for:** grounding, reducing sensory overload, calming the body quickly.

### 3.3.4 Physiological Sigh (Fastest Calm-Down)

1. Take a **short sharp inhale** through the nose
2. Immediately take a **second short inhale** on top of it (a "double inhale")
3. Follow with a **long, slow exhale** through the mouth
4. Repeat 1–3 times

**Best for:** immediate stress relief in under 30 seconds. Research by Andrew Huberman (Stanford) shows this is the fastest voluntary method to reduce physiological arousal.

### 3.3.5 Humming Breath (Vagus Nerve Activation)

1. Breathe **in** through the nose for **4 counts**
2. Breathe **out** while **humming** steadily for as long as comfortable
3. Repeat 5 times

**Best for:** vagus nerve stimulation, self-soothing, reducing heart rate. The vibration of humming directly stimulates the vagus nerve.

## 3.4 Emotional Regulation Strategies

Emotional regulation — the ability to manage, modify, and respond to emotional experiences — can be more challenging for neurodivergent individuals due to neurobiological differences, sensory sensitivities, and executive function challenges.

### 3.4.1 Understanding Emotional Dysregulation

Neurodivergent adults may experience:
- **Sensory overload** triggering intense emotional responses
- **Reduced interoception** — difficulty sensing internal body signals like a rising heart rate or shallow breathing, making it hard to recognise emotions before they become overwhelming
- **Alexithymia** — difficulty identifying and naming emotions (common in autism)
- **Rejection sensitivity** — heightened emotional reactions to perceived criticism or rejection
- **Emotional inertia** — getting "stuck" in an emotional state and finding it hard to shift
- **Meltdowns and shutdowns** — these are neurological events, not behavioural choices; they require compassion, not correction

### 3.4.2 The "Zones of Regulation" for Exercise

A simple framework for understanding your current state:

| Zone | How It Feels | Body Signs | What to Do |
|------|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Blue** (Low) | Sad, tired, bored, disconnected | Slow breathing, heavy limbs, low energy | Gentle alerting activities: brisk walk, upbeat music, cold water on face, crunchy snack |
| **Green** (Calm) | Calm, focused, happy, ready | Steady breathing, relaxed muscles | Ideal for exercise — proceed with your plan |
| **Yellow** (Heightened) | Anxious, frustrated, silly, excited | Faster breathing, fidgeting, tension | Calming activities first: breathing exercises, slow walk, reduce stimulation, then exercise gently |
| **Red** (Overwhelmed) | Angry, terrified, meltdown, panic | Racing heart, shallow breathing, trembling | Stop all activity. Move to a safe, quiet space. Use breathing techniques. Do not attempt exercise until Green or calm Yellow |

### 3.4.3 Building Interoception (Body Awareness)

Interoception is the ability to notice internal body signals — hunger, thirst, heart rate, muscle tension, temperature, needing the toilet. Many neurodivergent people have reduced interoception, which makes self-regulation harder.

**Exercises to build interoception:**
- **Body scan**: sit quietly and notice each body part from toes to head — what sensations do you feel?
- **Heart rate check**: place a hand on your chest before and after exercise — can you feel the difference?
- **Temperature check**: notice whether your hands feel warm or cool right now
- **Hunger/fullness scale**: rate your hunger on a scale of 1–10 before and after meals
- **Tension spotting**: where are you holding tension right now? Jaw? Shoulders? Hands?

Practise these daily — even 2 minutes of body scanning builds the skill over time.

### 3.4.4 Vagus Nerve Exercises for Emotional Regulation

The vagus nerve is the longest cranial nerve and is bidirectional — physical experiences like breathing, movement, and sound can meaningfully influence emotional states and stress recovery.

- **Cold exposure**: splash cold water on the face or hold an ice cube — activates the dive reflex and slows heart rate
- **Humming or singing**: vibrations stimulate the vagus nerve directly
- **Gargling**: vigorous gargling with water stimulates the vagal tone in the throat
- **Slow exhale breathing**: any breathing technique where the exhale is longer than the inhale
- **Gentle yoga or stretching**: particularly forward folds and twists
- **Social connection**: safe, warm social interaction activates the ventral vagal system

## 3.5 Motivation Strategies for Neurodivergent Individuals

Starting and maintaining an exercise habit can be challenging. Here are strategies that work well for neurodivergent individuals:

### 3.5.1 Make It Fun, Not a Chore

- Choose activities you **enjoy**, not what seems "correct"
- Add music, video, or a favourite show to exercise time
- Exercise can be anything — walking to a park, dancing in your room, playing a sport
- Gamify it: set personal records, use a tracking app, earn rewards

### 3.5.2 Use Visual Schedules and Rewards

- Write or draw your weekly exercise plan so you can see it
- Use a **reward chart** — put a sticker or checkmark for each session
- Celebrate small wins: finishing a 5-minute walk is a real achievement
- Take progress photos or keep an exercise journal

### 3.5.3 Start Tiny (The 2-Minute Rule)

- Begin with just **2 minutes** per day — so small it feels impossible to fail
- Once 2 minutes is consistent, try 5, then 10
- The goal is to build the **habit** first; duration comes later
- "I just have to put on my exercise shoes" is a valid starting goal

### 3.5.4 Exercise with a Friend or Caregiver

- Social exercise is more enjoyable for many people
- A caregiver or support worker can provide encouragement and safety
- Body-doubling (having someone present while you exercise) helps with task initiation — a well-documented ADHD/autism strategy

### 3.5.5 Same Time, Same Place

- Routine is comforting for many neurodivergent individuals
- Try to exercise at the same time each day — e.g., after breakfast, or before dinner
- Use a familiar space that feels safe and comfortable
- Pair exercise with an existing habit (e.g., "after I have my morning tea, I do 5 minutes of stretching")

### 3.5.6 Focus on How It Feels, Not How It Looks

- The goal is to feel good, not to look a certain way
- Notice how exercise makes you feel during and after — calmer, more focused, happier?
- These positive feelings are the real reward
- Keep a simple mood log: rate your mood 1–5 before and after exercise to see the pattern

### 3.5.7 Plan for Low-Motivation Days

- Have a **minimum viable workout** ready: "If I do nothing else, I will do 3 minutes of stretching"
- Recognise that motivation fluctuates — this is normal, not failure
- Use external accountability: a caregiver check-in, an alarm, a visual reminder
- Give yourself permission to do the easy version — movement is movement

## 3.6 Executive Function Support for Exercise

Executive function challenges affect planning, starting, switching, and finishing tasks. Up to 70% of autistic people experience executive function difficulties. Here are specific strategies:

### Task Initiation (Starting)

- Remove barriers: lay out exercise clothes the night before
- Use a visual countdown: "Exercise starts in 10 minutes"
- Pair with a trigger habit: "When my alarm goes off, I stand up and stretch"
- Body-doubling: have someone start exercising near you

### Task Switching (Transitioning Between Exercises)

- Use a written checklist or visual schedule of the workout
- Set a timer for each exercise so you know when to switch
- Keep the number of different exercises small (3–5 per session) to reduce cognitive load
- Minimise equipment changes between exercises

### Working Memory (Remembering the Plan)

- Print or display the workout on a screen at eye level
- Use an app or video that guides you through each step
- NuroCoach can text you a daily workout plan to follow

### Sustained Attention (Staying Focused)

- Keep sessions short: 15–20 minutes is effective
- Use music with a consistent beat to maintain rhythm
- Alternate between two exercises to prevent boredom
- Exercise in a distraction-free environment

## 3.7 Managing Exercise Anxiety

Some people feel nervous about exercise, especially in new environments or in front of others.

### Tips to Reduce Exercise Anxiety

- **Practice first at home** — learn new exercises in a safe, private space
- **Bring a support person** — a familiar caregiver can make new environments feel safer
- **Preview new places** — visit the gym or exercise space before the session to reduce surprises; ask for a quiet tour
- **Use noise-cancelling headphones** — if sound is a trigger
- **Communicate your needs** — NuroCoach can help you prepare what to say to instructors
- **Start with home workouts** — build confidence before trying group or gym settings
- **Use the physiological sigh** — take a double-inhale and long exhale before entering a new space

## 3.8 When to Seek Extra Support

NuroCoach is here to help, but some situations need professional support. Please reach out to a mental health professional or trusted person if:

- You feel persistently sad, hopeless, or empty for more than two weeks
- You have thoughts of harming yourself or others
- Anxiety is stopping you from leaving your home or doing daily activities
- You are using exercise excessively as a way to cope with difficult emotions
- You experience frequent meltdowns or shutdowns that feel unmanageable
